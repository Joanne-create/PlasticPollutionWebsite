Plastic Pollution is Destroying the World

Plastic pollution is the widespread contamination of natural environments by plastic materials that accumulate due to human activity. Made from synthetic polymers, plastics are incredibly resilient and designed to withstand long periods without decomposing, which contributes to their persistence in the environment. When disposed of improperly, plastics are carried by wind and water into various ecosystems. Larger plastics, like bottles and bags, can remain for hundreds of years, and over time, these break down into smaller fragments called microplastics, which are less than 5 millimeters in size.

Microplastics have now permeated nearly every corner of the planet, from the depths of the ocean to the peaks of remote mountains. Because these materials do not fully biodegrade, they remain in the environment indefinitely, creating a continuous cycle of pollution that is extremely difficult to reverse.

Furthermore, plastic pollution has more than doubled in the last 2 decades because of how cheap, sterile and useful it is, as it can be used on many different occasions, making plastic also very versatile. In the 1950s barely over two million tons of plastic was produced but in 2019 those numbers reached an astonishing amount of 450 million tonnes! This ongoing accumulation poses serious challenges to the health of ecosystems, economies, and communities.

Plastic was invented in the early 20th century as a wonder material that could mimic everything from silk to ivory. In 1907, Belgian chemist Leo Baekeland created the first fully synthetic plastic,Bakelite, which transformed manufacturing. Its ability to be molded into any shape, while being lightweight and durable, made it revolutionary. By the 1950s, plastic production boomed, giving rise to products like Tupperware, toys, and household goods..

The most common is polyethylene (PE), found in plastic bags, and polypropylene (PP), used in bottle caps. Polyvinyl chloride (PVC) is durable and used for pipes and flooring but can release toxic chemicals if not disposed of properly. Polystyrene (PS), used for foam cups and food containers, can leach harmful substances into food. Perhaps the most concerning are polycarbonate (PC) and certain forms of PVC, as they contain bisphenol A (BPA) and phthalates, which are known to disrupt hormones and may pose serious health risks. As we learn more about their impact, reducing toxic plastic use becomes crucial for our health and the environment.
