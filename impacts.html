What is Plastic Pollution affecting?

Plastic pollution poses significant risks to human health, primarily through the release of toxic chemicals. As plastic items degrade in landfills, they can leach harmful substances like BPA, phthalates, and dioxins into the soil and groundwater. When plastics are burned, they release toxic fumes that can cause respiratory problems and increase the risk of diseases like asthma and cancer. Moreover, microplastics have been discovered in the air, drinking water, and even in food. These tiny particles can accumulate in the human body over time, potentially leading to inflammation, organ damage, and other chronic health issues, although the full impact is still being studied.

Animals, particularly those in marine environments, are heavily impacted by plastic pollution. Every year, millions of birds, fish, and marine mammals die from ingesting plastic waste or getting entangled in discarded plastic items like fishing nets and plastic rings. Sea turtles often mistake floating plastic bags for jellyfish, which can block their digestive systems and lead to starvation. Even smaller creatures, like plankton, ingest microplastics, which then travel up the food chain, affecting larger animals and potentially humans. As a result, entire ecosystems are at risk as plastic pollution disrupts the balance of life in oceans, rivers, and coastal areas.

Plastic pollution severely impacts the environment by contaminating soil, waterways, and oceans. Once plastic waste enters ecosystems, it can take hundreds to thousands of years to break down, leading to long-lasting damage. In landfills, plastics can release chemicals that seep into the ground, affecting soil fertility and contaminating water sources. In oceans, plastic waste forms massive "garbage patches" like the Great Pacific Garbage Patch, which disrupt marine habitats and kill wildlife. As plastics fragment into microplastics, they become nearly impossible to remove, permanently altering natural environments and threatening both wildlife and human communities that depend on clean air, soil, and water.
